### Waseda University, Murakami Library

Dropped by Waseda University, where my main agenda was to visit Murakami's Library. Murakami was the first author that brought me into the world of Japanese literature. And so, I ought to pay homage to the man at his alumni. The entire space was designed by Kuma Kengo I learned.

![Murakami's Library From Outside](/assets/media/travel/murakami_lib_out.jpg)
*pardon my finger...*

The space was cosy, with a good selection of books and a peaceful atmosphere. Even the top floor was a space specifically curated with Murakami's possessions, along with his favourite selections of Jazz music (he used to run a Jazz bar known as *Peter Cat*). 

Part of me kept pretending I was inside one of his books. It worked.

<figure class="img-sm">
  <img src="/assets/media/travel/murakami_lib_up.jpg" alt="Murakami's Library From Inside">
</figure>

The library houses a collection of Murakami's works, including his novels, short stories, essays, and translations. Over 50 different translations, although I've only ever read his English translation (Oh, blimey!). 

There were also some works by other Japanese authors that I recognized, such as *Mieko Kawakami*, and she left a note in the library as well. Always thought that they had [*beef*](https://lithub.com/a-feminist-critique-of-murakami-novels-with-murakami-himself/). (*Maybe beef might be too strong a word*). 

Afterall, I guess this space is a living house where collaborations and friendships can be formed. Would definitely dive into the world of Japanese literature more after this trip.

Some that I've already added to my reading list after visiting the library:
- *No longer human* by Osamu Dazai
- *Temple of the golden pavilion* by Yukio Mishima
- *The Makioka sisters* by Junichiro Tanizaki
- *Kitchen* by Banana Yoshimoto

<p>Short History lesson: The Evolution of Sushi in A Chronological Journey</p>

<div class="timeline">

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-date">2nd–4th Century BC</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <h4>The Southeast Asian Root</h4>
      <p class="tl-era">Ancient Mekong River</p>
      <p><strong>The Innovation:</strong> Freshwater fish is gutted, salted, and packed into cooked rice.</p>
      <p><strong>The Purpose:</strong> Pure food preservation without refrigeration. Lactic acid from the fermenting rice stops bacteria from spoiling the fish over several months.</p>
      <p><strong>The Catch:</strong> The rice turns into a sour, foul-smelling paste and is thrown into the garbage. Only the preserved, sour fish is consumed.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-date">8th Century</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <h4>Narezushi Arrives in Japan</h4>
      <p class="tl-era">Nara Period (710–794)</p>
      <p><strong>The Transfer:</strong> The preservation method travels to Japan via China.</p>
      <p><strong>Cultural Adaptation:</strong> Known as Narezushi (aged sushi), it becomes a valuable commodity used for tax payments and temple offerings.</p>
      <p><strong>Modern Remnant:</strong> Funazushi (fermented crucian carp from Lake Biwa) is still made today, known for its intense, cheese-like aroma.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-date">17th Century</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <h4>The Rice Vinegar Shortcut</h4>
      <p class="tl-era">Edo Period (1603–1867)</p>
      <p><strong>The Innovation:</strong> Chefs grow tired of waiting months for fermentation and begin mixing fresh rice vinegar directly into warm rice.</p>
      <p><strong>The Shift:</strong> The vinegar provides an instant tangy, sour flavor, entirely bypassing the fermentation wait time.</p>
      <p><strong>The Result:</strong> Because the rice remains fresh and delicious rather than turning to mush, people finally start eating the rice and fish together (Haya-zushi).</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-date">1820s</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <h4>The Fast-Food Revolution</h4>
      <p class="tl-era">Late Edo Period</p>
      <p><strong>The Creator:</strong> A street-stall vendor named Hanaya Yohei operates in the bustling metropolis of Edo (modern Tokyo).</p>
      <p><strong>The Birth of Nigiri:</strong> He takes the vinegared rice, shapes it by hand, and tops it with fresh seafood caught right from Tokyo Bay.</p>
      <p><strong>The Concept:</strong> Sold as cheap, convenient street food for busy laborers. Original pieces were huge (the size of a slider), eaten with bare hands right at the cart.</p>
      <p><strong>Edomae Techniques:</strong> To prevent spoilage before refrigeration, chefs master marinating tuna in soy sauce (Zuke), curing mackerel in vinegar (Shime), and cooking eel (Anago).</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-date">1970s–1980s</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <h4>Global Boom &amp; High-End Standardization</h4>
      <p class="tl-era">Post-WWII &amp; Showa Era</p>
      <p><strong>Modern Tech:</strong> Refrigeration, indoor plumbing, and rapid transport allow sushi to move from outdoor street stalls into clean, indoor luxury counters.</p>
      <p><strong>Going Global:</strong> The California Roll in Los Angeles acts as a gateway drug for Westerners, turning sushi into an upscale international phenomenon.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-date">1990s</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <h4>The Birth of Omakase</h4>
      <p class="tl-era">The Japanese Bubble Economy</p>
      <p><strong>The Problem:</strong> Newly rich corporate workers flood premium sushi counters with massive budgets but zero knowledge of seasonal fish or traditional dining etiquette.</p>
      <p><strong>The Phrase:</strong> Customers begin using Omakase ("I leave it up to you") to hand total control to the chef.</p>
      <p><strong>The Legacy:</strong> This saves face for the customer, lets the chef showcase the absolute best catch of the day with zero waste, and permanently elevates sushi into a curated, theatrical performance.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

</div>

#### Some fun facts about the toilet project:
> The "Tokyo Toilet" project was launched by the Nippon Foundation ahead of the Tokyo 2020 Olympics to challenge negative public restroom stereotypes by introducing high-design, inclusive, and ultra-hygienic facilities. 

> Public toilets in Japan were notoriously known as the 4Ks, "Kurai" (dark), "Kitanai" (dirty), "Kowai" (scary), and "Kusai" (smelly). 

Then, project organization invited Wim Wenders to feature these toilets to give the project a narrative. 

Hey, hey, thanks to Wenders, I got to know about these toilets and went on a toilet hunting adventure in Tokyo!
